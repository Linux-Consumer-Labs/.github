# Contributing to Linux Consumer Labs

Thank you for helping us research a better Linux desktop experience. This organization values curiosity, rigor, and constructive dissent.

## Ways to Contribute

### 1. Propose Research

Use the **Research Proposal** issue template when you have a concrete topic worth investigating — a pain point, a cross-platform comparison, or a user-journey friction worth documenting.

Good research proposals include:
- A clear problem statement from a **consumer** perspective
- Why existing Linux approaches fall short (with evidence, not opinion alone)
- What other operating systems do and whether any of it is adaptable
- Expected deliverables (brief, prototype, comparison matrix, etc.)

### 2. Explore What-Ifs

Use the **What-If Exploration** issue template for speculative ideas. These do not need to be fully formed. They should be interesting enough to spark discussion and, potentially, a follow-up research track.

Examples:
- "What if the desktop handled driver installation the way ChromeOS handles peripheral pairing?"
- "What if we treated application state recovery like mobile OSes do?"

### 3. Write and Review

- Comment on open issues with evidence, lived experience, or links to prior art
- Submit pull requests that improve documentation, templates, or research briefs
- Keep tone respectful — we critique ideas and systems, not people

### 4. Build Prototypes

When a research track matures into a prototype, it typically moves to its own repository under the organization. Prototypes should be:
- **Small** — prove one idea, not rebuild a desktop
- **Honest** — document trade-offs and kernel boundaries
- **Reproducible** — include setup steps and known limitations

## Research Standards

We are exploratory, but not sloppy.

1. **Separate observation from recommendation.** Document what *is* before arguing what *should be*.
2. **Credit other platforms fairly.** Borrowing ideas is encouraged; misrepresenting them is not.
3. **Respect upstream and the kernel.** Proposals that require forked kernels or hostile forks of major projects need exceptional justification.
4. **Label uncertainty.** Use "hypothesis", "open question", and "needs validation" freely.
5. **Prefer reproducible examples.** Screenshots, logs, and version numbers beat vague anecdotes.

## Labels (Reference)

| Label | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| `research` | Active or proposed research track |
| `what-if` | Speculative exploration |
| `cross-platform` | Comparison with non-Linux OSes |
| `consumer-friction` | Everyday-user pain point |
| `needs-evidence` | Idea present, validation missing |
| `prototype-ready` | Mature enough for a spike repo |
